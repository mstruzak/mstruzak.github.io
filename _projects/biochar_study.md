Bioretention systems use soil media and vegetation to passively treat stormwater runoff. Biochar—a porous, carbon-rich material produced from the pyrolsis of organic matter—is sometimes used as a soil amendment for its ability to increase water retention, nutrient availability, carbon sequestration, and microbial activity, thus increasing soil health. Biochar's porosity and large surface area are also helpful for trapping both organic and inorganic contaminants. This study evaluated whether biochar could improve standard bioretention removal of metals, nutrients, and microplastics from stormwater.

We constructed nine pilot-scale columns with varying biochar ratios (0%, 25%, 50%) and ran five trials using stormwater collected from parking lot runoff on the University of Portland campus. Influent and effluent samples were analyzed for copper, zinc, nitrogen, phosphorus, and microplastics.

Results showed that biochar significantly improved metal removal: columns with 50% biochar achieved 79% zinc removal (vs. 54% for controls) and 73% copper removal (vs. 25% for controls). However, all columns, regardless of biochar content, showed consistent nutrient leaching, and microplastics removal (~86%) was unaffected by biochar content.

This work resulted in a first-author publication in the *Journal of Environmental Engineering* and was presented at various research showcases at the University of Portland, as well as Oregon's 2023 EWRI Sustainable Stormwater Symposium, where it won "Audience Favorite" for a $1,000 award.
