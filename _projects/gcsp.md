The [NAE Grand Challenge Scholars Program](https://gcspnetwork.org) is a multidisciplinary undergraduate program focused on preparing engineers to tackle complex global problems. My focus was on **Restoring and Improving Urban Infrastructure**, with an overarching theme of equitable engineering in cross-cultural settings.

I defined equitable engineering as the design and implementation of infrastructure solutions that minimize environmental impact, promote social equity, engage communities, and address ethical considerations. The program required developing five competencies through hands-on experiences:

### Social Consciousness
In 2022, during my sophomore year of college, I traveled to Anomabo, Ghana with the nonprofit, Global Brigades to help install biodigester toilet systems in the community of Suprudo, where residents of lack access to running water or sanitation infrastructure, and open defecation contributes to water-borne disease transmission. Working alongside local engineers and artisans, conversations with community members, underscored how directly infrastructure affects public health and wellbeing. This experience reinforced the importance of community engagement and culturally appropriate, context-specific design.

### Entrepreneurship
I studied Sustainable Energy Innovation the summer before my junior year at Reykjavik University in Reykjavik, Iceland, touring geothermal and hydroelectric facilities as well as carbon capture facilities. As part of a capstone project, my team developed a hypothetical business plan for ocean power implementation in U.S. coastal cities to reduce energy burden on low-income communities, identifying key resources, stakeholders, and economic constraints. This project emphasized that technical feasibility alone is insufficient—understanding cost structures and project lifecycle is essential for establishing sustainable infrastructure.

### Multicultural
I spent a semester at INSA Lyon in Lyon, France studying sustainable water resources, waste management, and urban planning. Beyond coursework, the city itself was instructive: integrated pedestrian infrastructure, urban gardens, and minimal car dependence offered a contrast to U.S. urban design. Engaging with students from France and across Europe highlighted diverse approaches to environmental challenges and reinforced that sustainability requires collective effort across cultural and disciplinary boundaries.

### Talent
While in Lyon, I completed an [independent research thesis on bioretention system resilience under climate change](https://mstruzak.github.io/projects/insa-these.html). I synthesized an in-depth literature review consisting of 84 peer-reviewed journal articles, compiled monitoring data from field-scale bioretention systems across multiple climates, developed a framework for evaluating system performance under future climate scenarios, and concucted prelimnary validation trials of the CANOE hydrologic model before defending the 65-page thesis before a faculty committee in French.

### Interdisciplinary
Infrastructure decisions directly affect communities, making ethical reasoning fundamental to practice. I examined multiple ethical frameworks—utilitarian, deontological, virtue, and care ethics, and how they apply to engineering decision-making. Integrating these perspectives is essential for equitable infrastructure development.

---

Participating in GCSP helped me tie together my undergraduate experiences into a cohesive whole. Having missed my first year of college in-person due to COVID-19, I was determined to make the most of my time, and this program pushed me to pursue opportunities I might otherwise have missed. It shaped my understanding of what it means to be a well-rounded engineer: one who balances technical skill with community engagement, cultural awareness, and ethical responsibility.
