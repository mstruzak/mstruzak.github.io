Ocean alkalinity enhancement (OAE) is a marine carbon dioxide removal strategy that involves adding alkaline minerals to seawater. This shifts the ocean's carbonate chemistry, converting dissolved CO₂ into stable bicarbonate and carbonate ions—allowing additional atmospheric CO₂ to be absorbed and stored long-term.

</p>

As part of the NASA JPL / Keck Institute Summer School on Satellite Observations and Climate Models, our team used an existing 1D ocean column model to evaluate OAE efficiency under different deployment scenarios, under JPL researcher, [Dr. Kay Suselj](https://science.jpl.nasa.gov/people/ksuselj/). We simulated alkalinity injections along a Pacific transect (165°W, 77°S to 52°N) and tested the effects of deployment timing (year-long vs. seasonal), latitude, vertical velocity, diffusivity, and sea ice coverage.
<p align="center">
  <img src="/assets/img/oae-transect.png" alt="OAE Deployment Transect" width="50%" />
    <br>
    <em>Simulated OAE deployments spaced 1° apart on transect 165°W between spaced between 77°S and 52°N.></em>
</p>

Key findings:
- **Efficiency varies by location and timing**: High-latitude deployments showed reduced efficiency due to upwelling and ice coverage, while mid-latitude deployments were most effective under year-long injection.
- **Efficiency takes years to stabilize**: Carbon removal continued to increase for several years after injection before reaching steady state.
- **Vertical velocity is the dominant control**: Upwelling zones significantly reduced efficiency by transporting alkalinity away from the surface before CO₂ equilibration could occur.

This work highlighted the importance of site selection and timing for OAE deployment, and the need for further research before real-world implementation.
